Content moderation is a necessary component of any online platform, such as social media, forums,
blogs or community platforms, to **ensure that users can interact in a safe and respectful manner**,
while also protecting the platform's reputation and complying with legal and ethical standards. 

Moderation simply consists in identifying, flagging and **filtering harmful or unwanted content**, 
such as hate speech, harassment or violence, across the digital platform. Screening can be performed 
before the content is published (pre-moderation), after the content is published (post-moderation), 
or through a combination of both approaches (hybrid moderation).

Screening and filtering can be done manually by human moderators or automatically through AI and 
Machine Learning algorithms.

## Manual Content Moderation

Manual moderation relies on human moderators to review and manage user-generated content on
a case-by-case basis. This approach can be effective for handling complex or nuanced content, but 
presents two main issues:

- It leads to **delays in content review**, which at best can result in a **poor user experience**, 
and at worst can allow harmful content to remain visible for extended periods of time. 

- It is **costly and time-consuming**, since it requires one or more human moderators to review each 
piece of content, which can be particularly challenging for smaller platforms or communities.

## Automated Content Moderation

Automated content moderation uses AI and Machine Learning algorithms to analyze and filter content
quickly and efficiently. This approach can be particularly effective for handling large volumes of
content, and can help to reduce the workload of human moderators. 

However, since automated moderation typically relies on user data being sent to **cloud-based AI 
models** or third-party APIs, it can raise **privacy concerns** as well as **issues 
with regulatory compliance**.

## The best of both worlds: Local Content Moderation with a Small Language Model

A third approach to content moderation which offers the best of both worlds is to use **Small 
Language Models that flags harmful content locally** without the need to send user data to external 
servers. 

Small Language Models are a scaled-down version of Large Language Models, which are designed to 
**perform specific tasks**, while being **thousands of times smaller in size** and computational
requirements compared to their larger counterparts. This makes them ideal for running on local
servers or even on edge devices, without the need for cloud-based processing.

This approach allows for **instant content analysis**, **enhanced privacy**, and **better control** 
over the moderation process.

At Tanaos we have developed a Small Language Model specifically designed for content moderation, 
which can be easily integrated into your platform to automatically flag harmful content in real-time.
**Data processing happens on your own servers**, ensuring that no user data is sent to external servers,
thus maintaining user privacy and complying with data protection regulations.
