Private, automated content moderation for blogs, forums and social media platforms

Automate content moderation and flag harmful content with a Small Language Model that runs locally for enhanced privacy and control.

March 20, 2026

Content moderation is a necessary component of any online platform, such as social media, forums, blogs and community platforms, to ensure that users can interact in a safe and respectful manner, while also protecting the platform's reputation and complying with legal and ethical standards.

Moderation simply consists in identifying, flagging and filtering harmful or unwanted content, such as hate speech, harassment or violence, across the digital platform. Screening can be performed before the content is published (pre-moderation), after the content is published (post-moderation), or through a combination of both approaches (hybrid moderation).

Screening and filtering can be done manually by human moderators or automatically through AI and Machine Learning algorithms.

Manual Content Moderation

Manual moderation relies on human moderators to review and manage user-generated content on a case-by-case basis. This approach can be effective for handling complex or nuanced content, but presents two main issues:

  • It leads to delays in content review, which at best can result in a poor user experience, and at worst can allow harmful content to remain visible for extended periods of time.

  • It is costly and time-consuming, since it requires one or more human moderators to review each piece of content, which can be particularly challenging for smaller platforms or communities.

Automated Content Moderation

Automated content moderation uses AI and Machine Learning algorithms to analyze and filter content quickly and efficiently. This approach can be particularly effective for handling large volumes of content, and can help to reduce the workload of human moderators.

However, since automated moderation typically relies on user data being sent to cloud-based AI models or third-party APIs, it can raise privacy concerns as well as issues with regulatory compliance.

The best of both worlds: Local Content Moderation with a Small Language Model

A third approach to content moderation which offers the best of both worlds is to use Small Language Models that flags harmful content locally without the need to send user data to external servers.

Small Language Models are a scaled-down version of Large Language Models, which are designed to perform specific tasks, while being thousands of times smaller in size and computational requirements compared to their larger counterparts. This makes them ideal for running on local servers or even on edge devices, without the need for cloud-based processing.

This approach allows for instant content analysis, enhanced privacy, and better control over the moderation process.

At Tanaos we have developed a Small Language Model specifically designed for content moderation, which can be easily integrated into your platform to automatically flag harmful content in real-time. Data processing happens on your own servers, ensuring that no user data is sent to external servers, thus maintaining user privacy and complying with data protection regulations.

Setting the model's safety threshold

Not all platforms require the same level of strictness in content moderation. Some platforms may want to be more tolerant when it comes to censoring content, while others may want to enforce stricter rules.

Let's feed our model with a mildly toxic sentence such as "I hate him":

from tanaos_guardrail_english import run_inference

result = run_inference(
  text="I hate him.",
  api_key="<YOUR_API_KEY>"
)
print(result)

Which results in:

[{'is_safe': True, 'scores': {'violence': 0.0495, 'non_violent_unethical': 0.01, 'hate_speech': 0.5326, 'financial_crime': 0.0021, 'discrimination': 0.0151, 'drug_weapons': 0.0007, 'self_harm': 0.0055, 'privacy': 0.0279, 'sexual_content': 0.0085, 'child_abuse': 0.0043, 'terrorism_organized_crime': 0.0021, 'hacking': 0.0008, 'animal_abuse': 0.0013, 'jailbreak_prompt_inj': 0.005}}]

By default, this sentence isn't flagged as unsafe. It is, after all, an expression of dislike targeting a specific individual, without any indication of violent or harmful intent. Depending on the type of tone we want to enforce on our platform, however, we may want to follow a stricter moderation policy.

If that's the case, we can get the Guardrail Model to be more severe by passing the safety_threshold argument. The default value of safety_threshold is 0.55 and the lower the value the stricter the moderation will be. Let's repeat the example with a lower safety_threshold value, such as 0.45:

from tanaos_guardrail_english import run_inference

result = run_inference(
  text="I hate him.",
  api_key="<YOUR_API_KEY>",
  safety_threshold=0.45
)
print(result)

Which results in:

[{'is_safe': False, 'scores': {'violence': 0.0495, 'non_violent_unethical': 0.01, 'hate_speech': 0.5326, 'financial_crime': 0.0021, 'discrimination': 0.0151, 'drug_weapons': 0.0007, 'self_harm': 0.0055, 'privacy': 0.0279, 'sexual_content': 0.0085, 'child_abuse': 0.0043, 'terrorism_organized_crime': 0.0021, 'hacking': 0.0008, 'animal_abuse': 0.0013, 'jailbreak_prompt_inj': 0.005}}]

With the new safety_threshold value, the sentence is now flagged as unsafe. So which value should you pick for safety_threshold? That really depends on how strict a moderation you want to perform. For most real-world purposes, we suggest not passing a safety_threshold value at all, which will use the default value of 0.55.

If a strict moderation is required, you can lower the safety_threshold to a values between 0.45 and 0.30. Anything below that will risk causing the model output many false positives. If a more lenient moderation is required, you can increase the safety_threshold to a value between 0.60 and 0.75. Anything above that will risk causing the model to output many false negatives.
